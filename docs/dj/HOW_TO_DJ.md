# How to DJ: Beginner Field Guide

This is a starter path for learning to DJ in a dance-music context. The goal is not to press sync and cosplay. The goal is to understand flow, timing, sound, and responsibility.

## 1. What a DJ actually does

A DJ designs a shared emotional arc using recorded music. The craft includes:

- Selection: choosing the right track for the room and moment.
- Timing: knowing when to enter, overlap, and leave.
- Mixing: blending tempo, phrase, EQ, and energy.
- Reading the room: watching bodies, not just waveforms.
- Stewardship: protecting the vibe, sound system, and people.

## 2. Basic gear

You can learn with:

- Laptop + DJ software.
- Controller with two decks, jog wheels, mixer, cue buttons, and EQ.
- Headphones for pre-cueing.
- Speakers or monitors at safe volume.
- Music files you are allowed to use.

Club-standard setups often use CDJs/media players and a mixer. Vinyl/DVS is another path.

## 3. The core controls

- **Decks:** where tracks play.
- **Mixer channels:** volume and tone for each deck.
- **Gain/trim:** input level before the channel fader.
- **EQ:** low/mid/high frequency control.
- **Filter:** sweep high-pass/low-pass style tone change.
- **Cue:** headphone preview.
- **Crossfader/channel faders:** blend tracks.
- **Pitch/tempo fader:** adjust BPM.
- **Jog wheel:** nudge timing or scratch.

## 4. Beatmatching

Beatmatching means aligning two tracks so their beats land together.

Practice drill:

1. Pick two house/techno tracks near the same BPM.
2. Play Track A to speakers.
3. Cue Track B in headphones.
4. Start Track B on the downbeat (“the one”).
5. Adjust tempo until both tracks run at the same speed.
6. Nudge Track B until kicks align.
7. Keep them locked for 32 bars.

Even if you use sync, learn this by ear. It teaches you to hear drift.

## 5. Phrasing

Most dance music is organized in bars and phrases. A common phrase length is 8, 16, or 32 bars. Big changes often happen at phrase boundaries: drums enter, bass drops, vocals start, breakdown begins.

Beginner rule:

- Start Track B at the beginning of a phrase in Track A.
- Mix intro drums over outro drums.
- Avoid bringing two full basslines or two lead vocals together unless you mean it.

## 6. EQ mixing

EQ is how you make space.

- **Lows:** kick and bass. Usually only one dominant low end at a time.
- **Mids:** vocals, snares, synth body. Clashes get messy here.
- **Highs:** hats, shimmer, air. Can fatigue ears if overdone.

Simple transition:

1. Bring Track B in with low EQ reduced.
2. Let highs/mids sit gently under Track A.
3. At a phrase boundary, swap the bass: reduce Track A lows, restore Track B lows.
4. Fade out Track A.

## 7. Set building

A set is a journey. Think in scenes:

- Opener: invite the room in.
- Lock-in: establish groove.
- Lift: raise energy.
- Peak: biggest pressure/release.
- Reset: give bodies a breath.
- Closing: leave them with a memory.

Tag tracks by energy, mood, key, BPM, and mix-in/mix-out notes.

## 8. Practice plan

### Week 1: timing
- Count beats and bars out loud.
- Drop tracks on the one.
- Beatmatch manually for 10 minutes/day.

### Week 2: phrasing
- Mark intro/outro sections.
- Do 32-bar blends.
- Record 15-minute practice mixes.

### Week 3: EQ
- Practice bass swaps.
- Practice vocal-safe transitions.
- Listen back on headphones and speakers.

### Week 4: mini-set
- Build a 30-minute arc.
- Record it in one take.
- Write notes: what worked, what dragged, what clashed.

## 9. Ethics

- Buy/support music when possible.
- Credit artists.
- Do not redline the mixer.
- Protect ears and gear.
- Read the room; do not punish the room with your ego.
- Make space for local/opening DJs.
