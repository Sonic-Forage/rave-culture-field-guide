# PLUR, Kandi, and Community Practice

## PLUR

**PLUR** stands for **Peace, Love, Unity, Respect**. In US rave history, it became a shorthand for dancefloor ethics.

- **Peace:** de-escalate, do not bring violence to the floor.
- **Love:** treat strangers as humans, not props.
- **Unity:** build collective energy without forcing sameness.
- **Respect:** consent, boundaries, culture, elders, newcomers, staff, locals.

PLUR is not a costume. It is a practice.

## Kandi

Kandi bracelets became a visible part of US rave and festival culture. Trading kandi can be a ritual of friendliness and memory. The point is not plastic beads; the point is a small exchange that says, “we shared this moment.”

Respect rules:

- Ask before touching someone’s kandi.
- Do not pressure people to trade.
- Do not mock newcomers.
- Do not use PLUR language to excuse creepy behavior.

## Underground vs mainstream

Rave culture has always moved between underground autonomy and mainstream spectacle.

Underground strengths:

- Lower barrier to experimentation.
- DIY art, sound, and community.
- Less dependence on brands.

Underground risks:

- Unsafe spaces, poor exits, lack of medical support.
- Gatekeeping and unaccountable organizers.
- Legal/noise/trespass conflicts.

Mainstream strengths:

- Professional production, permits, medical infrastructure.
- Artist career paths and access for large audiences.

Mainstream risks:

- Commercial extraction from underground culture.
- VIP/class division inside “unity” spaces.
- Over-policing, surveillance, brand flattening.

The goal is not to worship either side. The goal is to build better dancefloors.

## Etiquette for ravers

- Bring earplugs.
- Hydrate; help friends hydrate.
- Give dancers space.
- Do not film people in vulnerable states.
- Tip/support local artists and staff when possible.
- Learn the lineage before claiming the culture.
- Leave the space cleaner than you found it.
