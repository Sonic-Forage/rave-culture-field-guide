# Harm Reduction and Event Care

Harm reduction means reducing preventable harm without pretending risk does not exist. In rave culture, care is part of the culture, not an add-on.

## Universal event-care basics

- **Water:** easy access, clear signage, no punishment for hydration.
- **Cooling:** chill-out areas, airflow, shade, temperature monitoring.
- **Medical:** visible trained staff, escalation plan, radio/phone coverage.
- **Exits:** clear, unlocked, staffed, not bottlenecked.
- **Crowd flow:** avoid one-way crush traps; plan ingress/egress.
- **Hearing:** earplugs, reasonable sound levels, breaks from front-stack pressure.
- **Consent:** anti-harassment policy, bystander support, accountable security.
- **Accessibility:** seating, mobility access, sensory rest options where possible.

## Drug-checking context

Some harm-reduction organizations provide reagent testing, fentanyl test strips, or lab-based drug checking where legal. This repo does not tell people to use drugs. It explains why safety advocates argue that accurate information and emergency readiness save lives.

## Naloxone

Naloxone reverses opioid overdose and is increasingly present at music events because fentanyl contamination can appear outside expected opioid contexts. Availability and legality vary by location.

## Heat stress warning signs

- Confusion, fainting, inability to cool down.
- Hot skin, stopped sweating, severe headache.
- Vomiting, seizure, collapse.

Treat these as emergency signals. Get medical help immediately.

## Consent culture checklist

- “No” is complete.
- Intoxication is not consent.
- Do not touch strangers’ bodies, hair, kandi, props, or outfits without permission.
- Look out for people being isolated or pressured.
- Security should protect attendees, not profile or intimidate them.

## The rave care principle

A scene is not mature because it is hard, secret, or exclusive. It is mature when people can enter, dance, leave, and recover without being exploited, harmed, or erased.
